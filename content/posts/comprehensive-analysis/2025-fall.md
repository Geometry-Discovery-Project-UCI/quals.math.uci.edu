::ProblemBlock{number=1}
#problem
Let $f_n:[0,1]\to\mathbb R$ be a sequence of continuous functions that
converges uniformly to $f$. Show that
$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}\int_0^1 \sqrt{1+f_n(x)^2}\,dx
=
\int_0^1 \sqrt{1+f(x)^2}\,dx.
$$

#proof
Let
$$
\Phi(t)=\sqrt{1+t^2}.
$$
Then $\Phi$ is Lipschitz with constant $1$ on $\mathbb R$, since
$$
|\Phi'(t)|=\frac{|t|}{\sqrt{1+t^2}}\le 1.
$$
Hence, for all $a,b\in\mathbb R$,
$$
|\Phi(a)-\Phi(b)|\le |a-b|.
$$
Therefore,
$$
\left|
\int_0^1 \sqrt{1+f_n(x)^2}\,dx
-
\int_0^1 \sqrt{1+f(x)^2}\,dx
\right|
\le
\int_0^1 |f_n(x)-f(x)|\,dx.
$$
Since $f_n\to f$ uniformly on $[0,1]$,
$$
\int_0^1 |f_n(x)-f(x)|\,dx
\le
\|f_n-f\|_\infty\to0.
$$
Thus
$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}\int_0^1 \sqrt{1+f_n(x)^2}\,dx
=
\int_0^1 \sqrt{1+f(x)^2}\,dx.
$$

::ProblemBlock{number=2}
#problem
Suppose $f:(0,\infty)\to\mathbb R$ is continuously differentiable and
$$
\lim_{x\to\infty} f'(x)=L\in\mathbb R.
$$
Define
$$
g(x)=f(x+1)-f(x).
$$
Prove that
$$
\lim_{x\to\infty} g(x)=L.
$$


#proof
By the fundamental theorem of calculus,
$$
g(x)=f(x+1)-f(x)=\int_x^{x+1} f'(t)\,dt.
$$
Therefore,
$$
g(x)-L
=
\int_x^{x+1} f'(t)\,dt-\int_x^{x+1}L\,dt
=
\int_x^{x+1}(f'(t)-L)\,dt.
$$
Hence
$$
|g(x)-L|
\le
\int_x^{x+1}|f'(t)-L|\,dt.
$$
Since $f'(t)\to L$ as $t\to\infty$, for every $\varepsilon>0$, there exists
$R>0$ such that if $t\ge R$, then
$$
|f'(t)-L|<\varepsilon.
$$
Thus if $x\ge R$, then $t\in[x,x+1]$ implies $t\ge R$, and so
$$
|g(x)-L|
\le
\int_x^{x+1}\varepsilon\,dt
=
\varepsilon.
$$
Therefore
$$
\lim_{x\to\infty}g(x)=L.
$$

::ProblemBlock{number=3}
#problem
Let $K\subset\mathbb R$ be compact, and suppose $f_n:K\to\mathbb R$ is a
sequence of continuous functions converging uniformly to $f$. Show that $f$ is
continuous.

#proof
Let $x_0\in K$ and let $\varepsilon>0$. Since $f_n\to f$ uniformly on $K$,
there exists $N\in\mathbb N$ such that
$$
\|f_N-f\|_\infty<\frac{\varepsilon}{3}.
$$
Since $f_N$ is continuous at $x_0$, there exists $\delta>0$ such that if
$x\in K$ and $|x-x_0|<\delta$, then
$$
|f_N(x)-f_N(x_0)|<\frac{\varepsilon}{3}.
$$
Therefore, if $x\in K$ and $|x-x_0|<\delta$, then
$$
|f(x)-f(x_0)|
\le
|f(x)-f_N(x)|
+
|f_N(x)-f_N(x_0)|
+
|f_N(x_0)-f(x_0)|.
$$
Hence
$$
|f(x)-f(x_0)|
<
\frac{\varepsilon}{3}
+
\frac{\varepsilon}{3}
+
\frac{\varepsilon}{3}
=
\varepsilon.
$$
Thus $f$ is continuous at $x_0$. Since $x_0\in K$ was arbitrary, $f$ is
continuous on $K$.

::ProblemBlock{number=4}
#problem
Define $f:[-1,1]\to\mathbb R$ by
$$
f(x)=
\begin{cases}
\sin x, & x\in\mathbb Q\cap[-1,1],\\
0, & x\notin\mathbb Q\cap[-1,1].
\end{cases}
$$
Determine whether $f$ is Riemann integrable on $[-1,1]$ and justify the answer.


#proof
We claim that $f$ is not Riemann integrable on $[-1,1]$.

First, $f$ is continuous at $x=0$. Indeed, if $x\in\mathbb Q$, then
$f(x)=\sin x\to0$, and if $x\notin\mathbb Q$, then $f(x)=0$. Hence
$$
\lim_{x\to0}f(x)=0=f(0).
$$

Now let $x_0\ne0$. Choose a sequence of rational numbers $r_n\to x_0$ and a
sequence of irrational numbers $s_n\to x_0$. Then
$$
f(r_n)=\sin r_n\to \sin x_0,
$$
while
$$
f(s_n)=0\to0.
$$
Since $x_0\ne0$ and $x_0\in[-1,1]$, we have $\sin x_0\ne0$. Thus the two
limits are different, so $f$ is discontinuous at every $x_0\ne0$.

Therefore the set of discontinuities of $f$ is
$$
[-1,1]\setminus\{0\},
$$
which has positive Lebesgue measure. By the Lebesgue criterion for Riemann
integrability, a bounded function on a compact interval is Riemann integrable
if and only if its set of discontinuities has measure zero. Hence $f$ is not
Riemann integrable on $[-1,1]$.

::ProblemBlock{number=5}
#problem
Let $f:\mathbb R^2\to\mathbb R$ be differentiable and suppose there exists a
constant $M>0$ such that
$$
\|\nabla f(x,y)\|\le M
$$
for all $(x,y)\in\mathbb R^2$. Prove that $f$ is Lipschitz continuous, namely
for all $(x,y),(a,b)\in\mathbb R^2$,
$$
|f(x,y)-f(a,b)|
\le
M\sqrt{(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2}.
$$

#proof
 Fix $(x,y),(a,b)\in\mathbb R^2$. Define
$$
\gamma(t)=(a,b)+t((x,y)-(a,b)),
\qquad 0\le t\le1.
$$
Thus
$$
\gamma(t)=(a+t(x-a), b+t(y-b)).
$$
Define
$$
\phi(t)=f(\gamma(t)).
$$
Then $\phi$ is differentiable and
$$
\phi'(t)=\nabla f(\gamma(t))\cdot((x-a),(y-b)).
$$
By the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality,
$$
|\phi'(t)|
\le
\|\nabla f(\gamma(t))\|\sqrt{(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2}
\le
M\sqrt{(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2}.
$$
Therefore,
$$
|f(x,y)-f(a,b)|
=
|\phi(1)-\phi(0)|
\le
\int_0^1 |\phi'(t)|\,dt.
$$
Hence
$$
|f(x,y)-f(a,b)|
\le
M\sqrt{(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2}.
$$
Thus $f$ is Lipschitz continuous with Lipschitz constant $M$.
::

 

::ProblemBlock{number=6}
#problem
A function $f:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R$ is lower semi-continuous at $a\in\mathbb R$
if for all $\varepsilon>0$, there exists $\delta>0$ such that, for all
$x\in\mathbb R$, if $|a-x|<\delta$, then
$$
f(x)\ge f(a)-\varepsilon.
$$
The function $f$ is lower semi-continuous if it is lower semi-continuous at
every $a\in\mathbb R$.


<span style="display:inline-block; width:1em;"></span> **(a)** Prove that $f$ is lower semi-continuous if and only if
$$
\{(x,y)\in\mathbb R^2:y\ge f(x)\}
$$
is closed.

<span style="display:inline-block; width:1em;"></span> **(b)** If $f_n:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R$ are lower semi-continuous functions, show
that
$$
f=\sup_n f_n
$$
is also lower semi-continuous.

<span style="display:inline-block; width:1em;"></span> **(c)** If $f$ is lower semi-continuous and bounded from below, show that $f$
attains a minimum value on any interval $[a,b]$.


#proof
<span style="display:inline-block; width:1em;"></span> **(a)**
Let
$$
\operatorname{epi}(f)=\{(x,y)\in\mathbb R^2:y\ge f(x)\}.
$$

First assume that $f$ is lower semi-continuous. Let
$$
(x_n,y_n)\in\operatorname{epi}(f)
$$
and suppose
$$
(x_n,y_n)\to(x,y).
$$
We need to show that $(x,y)\in\operatorname{epi}(f)$, that is,
$$
y\ge f(x).
$$
Since $f$ is lower semi-continuous at $x$, for every $\varepsilon>0$, there
exists $\delta>0$ such that if $|z-x|<\delta$, then
$$
f(z)\ge f(x)-\varepsilon.
$$
For $n$ sufficiently large, $|x_n-x|<\delta$, so
$$
f(x_n)\ge f(x)-\varepsilon.
$$
Since $(x_n,y_n)\in\operatorname{epi}(f)$, we have $y_n\ge f(x_n)$. Thus
$$
y_n\ge f(x)-\varepsilon
$$
for all sufficiently large $n$. Letting $n\to\infty$, we get
$$
y\ge f(x)-\varepsilon.
$$
Since $\varepsilon>0$ was arbitrary,
$$
y\ge f(x).
$$
Hence $\operatorname{epi}(f)$ is closed.

Conversely, suppose $\operatorname{epi}(f)$ is closed. We prove that $f$ is
lower semi-continuous. Suppose not. Then there exist $a\in\mathbb R$ and
$\varepsilon>0$ such that for every $\delta>0$, there exists $x$ with
$|x-a|<\delta$ and
$$
f(x)<f(a)-\varepsilon.
$$
Thus we can choose a sequence $x_n\to a$ such that
$$
f(x_n)<f(a)-\varepsilon.
$$
Let
$$
y=f(a)-\frac{\varepsilon}{2}.
$$
Then
$$
y>f(x_n)
$$
for every $n$, so
$$
(x_n,y)\in\operatorname{epi}(f).
$$
Since $(x_n,y)\to(a,y)$ and $\operatorname{epi}(f)$ is closed, we get
$$
(a,y)\in\operatorname{epi}(f).
$$
Therefore
$$
y\ge f(a).
$$
But $y=f(a)-\frac{\varepsilon}{2}<f(a)$, a contradiction. Hence $f$ is lower
semi-continuous.

<span style="display:inline-block; width:1em;"></span> **(b)**
For each $n$, since $f_n$ is lower semi-continuous,
$$
\operatorname{epi}(f_n)=\{(x,y):y\ge f_n(x)\}
$$
is closed. Now
$$
f(x)=\sup_n f_n(x).
$$
Thus
$$
y\ge f(x)
$$
if and only if
$$
y\ge f_n(x)
$$
for every $n$. Therefore
$$
\operatorname{epi}(f)
=
\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty}\operatorname{epi}(f_n).
$$
Since an intersection of closed sets is closed, $\operatorname{epi}(f)$ is
closed. By part (a), $f$ is lower semi-continuous.

<span style="display:inline-block; width:1em;"></span> **(c)**
Let $[a,b]$ be a compact interval. Since $f$ is bounded from below, define
$$
m=\inf_{x\in[a,b]} f(x)>-\infty.
$$
Choose a sequence $x_n\in[a,b]$ such that
$$
f(x_n)\to m.
$$
Since $[a,b]$ is compact, there exists a subsequence $x_{n_j}$ and a point
$x_0\in[a,b]$ such that
$$
x_{n_j}\to x_0.
$$
By lower semi-continuity,
$$
\liminf_{j\to\infty} f(x_{n_j})\ge f(x_0).
$$
Since $f(x_{n_j})\to m$, we get
$$
f(x_0)\le m.
$$
But by definition of $m$,
$$
m\le f(x_0).
$$
Therefore
$$
f(x_0)=m.
$$
Hence $f$ attains its minimum value on $[a,b]$.
::

::ProblemBlock{number=7}
#problem
Let $\{f_n\}$ be a sequence of continuous functions $[0,1]\to\mathbb R$ such
that for some $M>0$,
$$
\int_0^1 |f_n(t)|^2\,dt\le M
$$
for all $n\in\mathbb N$. Define $g_n:[0,1]\to\mathbb R$ by
$$
g_n(x)=\int_0^1 \sqrt{x+t}\, f_n(t)\,dt.
$$
Show that there exists a subsequence of $\{g_n\}$ that converges uniformly on
$[0,1]$.

#proof
We prove that $\{g_n\}$ is uniformly bounded and equicontinuous. Then the
[Arzela-Ascoli theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arzelà–Ascoli_theorem) gives a uniformly convergent subsequence.

First, by the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality,
$$
|g_n(x)|
\le
\left(\int_0^1 (x+t)\,dt\right)^{1/2}
\left(\int_0^1 |f_n(t)|^2\,dt\right)^{1/2}.
$$
Since $0\le x\le1$,
$$
\int_0^1 (x+t)\,dt=x+\frac12\le \frac32.
$$
Also,
$$
\int_0^1 |f_n(t)|^2\,dt\le M.
$$
Therefore
$$
|g_n(x)|\le \sqrt{\frac{3M}{2}}
$$
for all $x\in[0,1]$ and all $n$.

Now let $x,y\in[0,1]$. Again by Cauchy-Schwarz,
$$
|g_n(x)-g_n(y)|
\le
\left(\int_0^1 |\sqrt{x+t}-\sqrt{y+t}|^2\,dt\right)^{1/2}
\left(\int_0^1 |f_n(t)|^2\,dt\right)^{1/2}.
$$
For nonnegative $u,v$,
$$
|\sqrt u-\sqrt v|\le \sqrt{|u-v|}.
$$
Thus
$$
|\sqrt{x+t}-\sqrt{y+t}|^2\le |x-y|.
$$
Hence
$$
\int_0^1 |\sqrt{x+t}-\sqrt{y+t}|^2\,dt
\le
|x-y|.
$$
Therefore
$$
|g_n(x)-g_n(y)|
\le
\sqrt{M}|x-y|^{1/2}.
$$
This bound is independent of $n$, so $\{g_n\}$ is equicontinuous.

Thus $\{g_n\}$ is uniformly bounded and equicontinuous on the compact interval
$[0,1]$. By the Arzela-Ascoli theorem, there exists a subsequence of $\{g_n\}$
that converges uniformly on $[0,1]$.
::

::ProblemBlock{number=8}
#problem
Let $f:[a,b]\to\mathbb R$ be a continuous function. Prove that, for any
non-decreasing continuous function $G:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R$, we have
$$
\left(\int_a^b f(x)G(f(x))\,dx\right)
\left(\int_a^b dx\right)
\ge
\left(\int_a^b G(f(x))\,dx\right)
\left(\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\right).
$$
Moreover, if $G$ is strictly increasing, prove that equality holds if and only
if $f$ is a constant function.

#proof
Let
$$
L=b-a.
$$
We compute
$$
L\int_a^b f(x)G(f(x))\,dx
-
\left(\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\right)
\left(\int_a^b G(f(x))\,dx\right).
$$
Using double integrals, this equals
$$
\int_a^b\int_a^b f(x)G(f(x))\,dx\,dy
-
\int_a^b\int_a^b f(x)G(f(y))\,dx\,dy.
$$
Symmetrizing, we obtain
$$
L\int_a^b f(x)G(f(x))\,dx
-
\left(\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\right)
\left(\int_a^b G(f(x))\,dx\right)
$$
$$
=
\frac12
\int_a^b\int_a^b
(f(x)-f(y))(G(f(x))-G(f(y)))\,dx\,dy.
$$
Since $G$ is non-decreasing, for every $x,y\in[a,b]$,
$$
(f(x)-f(y))(G(f(x))-G(f(y)))\ge0.
$$
Therefore
$$
\frac12
\int_a^b\int_a^b
(f(x)-f(y))(G(f(x))-G(f(y)))\,dx\,dy
\ge0.
$$
Hence
$$
\left(\int_a^b f(x)G(f(x))\,dx\right)
\left(\int_a^b dx\right)
\ge
\left(\int_a^b G(f(x))\,dx\right)
\left(\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\right).
$$

Now assume that $G$ is strictly increasing. If $f$ is constant, then equality is
immediate.

Conversely, suppose equality holds. Then
$$
\int_a^b\int_a^b
(f(x)-f(y))(G(f(x))-G(f(y)))\,dx\,dy
=
0.
$$
The integrand is continuous and nonnegative. Hence it must be identically zero:
$$
(f(x)-f(y))(G(f(x))-G(f(y)))=0
$$
for all $x,y\in[a,b]$.
Since $G$ is strictly increasing, if $f(x)\ne f(y)$, then
$$
(f(x)-f(y))(G(f(x))-G(f(y)))>0,
$$
which is impossible. Thus
$$
f(x)=f(y)
$$
for all $x,y\in[a,b]$. Therefore $f$ is constant.

::ProblemBlock{number=9}
#problem
Let
$$
B_n(r)=\{(x_1,\dots,x_n)\in\mathbb R^n:x_1^2+\cdots+x_n^2\le r^2\}
$$
denote the closed ball with radius $r$. Evaluate the following integral:
$$
\int\cdots\int_{B_n(r)}
(x_1^2+\cdots+x_n^2)^{3/2}\,dx_1\cdots dx_n.
$$
Recall that the volume of $B_n(r)$ is
$$
|B_n(r)|
=
\frac{\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma(n/2+1)}r^n.
$$

#proof
Let
$$
\omega_n=|B_n(1)|
=
\frac{\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma(n/2+1)}.
$$
In polar coordinates in $\mathbb R^n$,
$$
x_1^2+\cdots+x_n^2=\rho^2.
$$
Thus
$$
(x_1^2+\cdots+x_n^2)^{3/2}=\rho^3.
$$
The volume element is
$$
dx=n\omega_n \rho^{n-1}\,d\rho,
$$
because the surface area of the unit sphere in $\mathbb R^n$ is $n\omega_n$.
Therefore
$$
\int\cdots\int_{B_n(r)}
(x_1^2+\cdots+x_n^2)^{3/2}\,dx_1\cdots dx_n
=
\int_0^r \rho^3 n\omega_n \rho^{n-1}\,d\rho.
$$
Hence
$$
\int\cdots\int_{B_n(r)}
(x_1^2+\cdots+x_n^2)^{3/2}\,dx_1\cdots dx_n
=
n\omega_n\int_0^r \rho^{n+2}\,d\rho.
$$
Thus
$$
\int\cdots\int_{B_n(r)}
(x_1^2+\cdots+x_n^2)^{3/2}\,dx_1\cdots dx_n
=
n\omega_n\frac{r^{n+3}}{n+3}.
$$
Substituting
$$
\omega_n=\frac{\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma(n/2+1)},
$$
we get
$$
\boxed{
\int\cdots\int_{B_n(r)}
(x_1^2+\cdots+x_n^2)^{3/2}\,dx_1\cdots dx_n
=
\frac{n}{n+3}
\frac{\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma(n/2+1)}
r^{n+3}.
}
$$
