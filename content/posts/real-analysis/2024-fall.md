::ProblemBlock{number=1}
#problem
Let $f$ be a positive continuous function on $[0,1]$. Prove that 
$$
\lim_{r\to-\infty}\left(\int_0^1 f^r\right)^{1/r}=\min_{[0,1]} f.
$$

#proof
Let
$$
m=\min_{[0,1]} f.
$$
Since $f$ is positive and continuous on $[0,1]$, we have $m>0$.

For any $\eps>0$, let
$$
E=\{x\in [0,1]\mid f(x)<m+\eps\}.
$$
By continuity of $f$, $\mu(E)>0$. We also have
$$
\int_0^1 f^r\geq \int_E f^r\geq\mu(E)(m+\eps)^r.
$$
Thus we have
$$
\mu(E)^{1/r}(m+\eps)\geq \left(\int_0^1 f^r\right)^{1/r}\geq m.
$$
Letting $r\to-\infty$, we get
$$
m+\eps\geq \lim_{r\to-\infty}\left(\int_0^1 f^r\right)^{1/r}\geq m.
$$
Since $\eps$ is arbitrary, we complete the proof.

::ProblemBlock{number=2}
#problem
Suppose that $f\in L^1(\mathbb{R})$. Show that
$$
\lim_{n\to\infty} f(x+n)=0 \quad \text{for \ a.e.\,}\,\, x\in\mathbb{R}
$$
Here $n=1,2,3,\cdots$.


#proof
It is enough to prove the statement on each interval $[m,m+1]$, where
$m\in\mathbb Z$, and then take a countable union.

Fix $m\in\mathbb Z$. Consider
$$
\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} |f(x+n)|.
$$
By [Tonelli's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fubini%27s_theorem#Tonelli's_theorem)
$$
\int_m^{m+1} \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} |f(x+n)|\,dx
=
\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \int_m^{m+1} |f(x+n)|\,dx.
$$
Using the change of variables $y=x+n$, we get
$$
\int_m^{m+1} |f(x+n)|\,dx
=
\int_{m+n}^{m+n+1} |f(y)|\,dy.
$$
Hence
$$
\int_m^{m+1} \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} |f(x+n)|\,dx
=
\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}
\int_{m+n}^{m+n+1} |f(y)|\,dy
=
\int_{m+1}^{\infty} |f(y)|\,dy.
$$
Since $f\in L^1(\mathbb R)$, the last integral is finite. Therefore
$$
\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} |f(x+n)|<\infty
$$
for almost every $x\in[m,m+1]$.

For such $x$, the series
$$
\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} |f(x+n)|
$$
converges, so its terms must tend to $0$. Thus
$$
|f(x+n)|\to 0,
$$
and therefore
$$
f(x+n)\to 0
$$
for almost every $x\in[m,m+1]$.

Finally, since
$$
\mathbb R=\bigcup_{m\in\mathbb Z} [m,m+1],
$$
and this is a countable union, the conclusion holds for almost every
$x\in\mathbb R$. Hence
$$
\lim_{n\to\infty} f(x+n)=0
\qquad\text{for a.e. }x\in\mathbb R.
$$

::ProblemBlock{number=3}
#problem
Fix $p>1$, and suppose that $u\in L^p([0,1])$ is nonnegative and that
$$
\int_{\{u\ge t\}} u\,dx \le \min\{1,t^{-p}\}
$$
for all $t>0$. Prove that
$$
\int_0^1 u^p\,dx \le p.
$$

#proof
Assume that for every $t>0$,
$$
\int_{\{u\ge t\}} u\,dx \le \min\{1,t^{-p}\}.
$$
We use the layer-cake identity
$$
u(x)^p
=
(p-1)\int_0^{u(x)} t^{p-2}u(x)\,dt.
$$
Indeed,
$$
(p-1)\int_0^{u(x)} t^{p-2}u(x)\,dt
=
(p-1)u(x)\frac{u(x)^{p-1}}{p-1}
=
u(x)^p.
$$
Therefore, by [Tonelli's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fubini%27s_theorem#Tonelli's_theorem)
$$
\int_0^1 u(x)^p\,dx
=
(p-1)\int_0^1\int_0^{u(x)} t^{p-2}u(x)\,dt\,dx.
$$
Interchanging the order of integration gives
$$
\int_0^1 u(x)^p\,dx
=
(p-1)\int_0^\infty t^{p-2}
\int_{\{u\ge t\}} u(x)\,dx\,dt.
$$
Using the hypothesis,
$$
\int_0^1 u(x)^p\,dx
\le
(p-1)\int_0^\infty t^{p-2}\min\{1,t^{-p}\}\,dt.
$$
Now split the integral at $t=1$:
$$
\int_0^\infty t^{p-2}\min\{1,t^{-p}\}\,dt
=
\int_0^1 t^{p-2}\,dt
+
\int_1^\infty t^{p-2}t^{-p}\,dt.
$$
Since $p>1$,
$$
\int_0^1 t^{p-2}\,dt
=
\frac{1}{p-1},
$$
and
$$
\int_1^\infty t^{-2}\,dt
=
1.
$$
Hence
$$
\int_0^1 u(x)^p\,dx
\le
(p-1)\left(\frac{1}{p-1}+1\right)
=
p.
$$
Therefore,
$$
\int_0^1 u^p\,dx\le p.
$$
::

::ProblemBlock{number=4}
#problem
Let
$$
g(x)=
\begin{cases}
1, & x\in [k,k+2/3],\ k\in\mathbb Z,\\[2mm]
-1, & \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
$$
and let $g_j(x)=g(jx)$, $j\in\mathbb N$. Prove that for all
$f\in L^1([0,1])$,
$$
\lim_{j\to\infty}\int_0^1 g_j(x)f(x)\,dx
=
\frac13\int_0^1 f(x)\,dx.
$$


#proof
First observe that $g$ is periodic with period $1$, and
$$
\int_0^1 g(x)\,dx
=
\int_0^{2/3} 1\,dx+\int_{2/3}^1 (-1)\,dx
=
\frac23-\frac13
=
\frac13.
$$
Define
$$
h(x)=g(x)-\frac13.
$$
Then $h$ is also periodic with period $1$, and
$$
\int_0^1 h(x)\,dx=0.
$$
It is enough to prove that
$$
\lim_{j\to\infty}\int_0^1 h(jx)f(x)\,dx=0.
$$

We first prove this when $f=\mathbf 1_{[a,b]}$. Define
$$
H(t)=\int_0^t h(s)\,ds.
$$
Since $h$ has period $1$ and mean zero over one period, $H$ is bounded on
$\mathbb R$. Therefore,
$$
\int_a^b h(jx)\,dx
=
\frac1j \int_{ja}^{jb} h(y)\,dy
=
\frac1j\bigl(H(jb)-H(ja)\bigr).
$$
Since $H$ is bounded, it follows that
$$
\lim_{j\to\infty}\int_a^b h(jx)\,dx=0.
$$
By linearity, the same conclusion holds for every step function $\varphi$ on
$[0,1]$:
$$
\lim_{j\to\infty}\int_0^1 h(jx)\varphi(x)\,dx=0.
$$

Now let $f\in L^1([0,1])$. Since step functions are dense in
$L^1([0,1])$, for every $\varepsilon>0$ there exists a step function
$\varphi$ such that
$$
\|f-\varphi\|_{L^1}<\varepsilon.
$$
Also, since $h$ is bounded,
$$
\|h\|_\infty \le \frac43.
$$
Hence
$$
\left|\int_0^1 h(jx)(f(x)-\varphi(x))\,dx\right|
\le
\|h\|_\infty \|f-\varphi\|_{L^1}
\le
\frac43\varepsilon.
$$
Therefore,
$$
\limsup_{j\to\infty}
\left|\int_0^1 h(jx)f(x)\,dx\right|
\le
\frac43\varepsilon.
$$
Since $\varepsilon>0$ is arbitrary,
$$
\lim_{j\to\infty}\int_0^1 h(jx)f(x)\,dx=0.
$$

Finally, because $h(x)=g(x)-\frac13$, we have
$$
g(jx)=h(jx)+\frac13.
$$
Thus
$$
\int_0^1 g(jx)f(x)\,dx
=
\int_0^1 h(jx)f(x)\,dx
+
\frac13\int_0^1 f(x)\,dx.
$$
Taking $j\to\infty$, we obtain
$$
\lim_{j\to\infty}\int_0^1 g_j(x)f(x)\,dx
=
\frac13\int_0^1 f(x)\,dx.
$$

::ProblemBlock{number=5}
#problem
Show that there does not exist a function $g\in L^1(\mathbb R)$ such that
$$
f*g=f
$$
for all $f\in L^1(\mathbb R)$. Here
$$
f*g(x)=\int_{\mathbb R} f(y)g(x-y)\,dy
$$
is the convolution of $f$ and $g$.

#proof
 Suppose, for contradiction, that such a function $g\in L^1(\mathbb R)$ exists.

Take
$$
f=\mathbf 1_{[0,1]}.
$$
Then $f\in L^1(\mathbb R)$, so by assumption,
$$
f*g=f.
$$
However, since both $f$ and $g$ belong to $L^1(\mathbb R)$, the convolution
$f*g$ is a continuous function on $\mathbb R$.

Thus $f*g$ is continuous and equals $\mathbf 1_{[0,1]}$ almost everywhere. We
claim this is impossible.

Indeed, if a continuous function $h$ equals $\mathbf 1_{[0,1]}$ almost
everywhere, then $h=1$ on $(0,1)$ and $h=0$ on $(-\infty,0)\cup(1,\infty)$.
This follows from continuity: if, for example, $h(x_0)\ne 1$ for some
$x_0\in(0,1)$, then by continuity $h$ would differ from $1$ on an interval of
positive measure, contradicting equality almost everywhere.

Therefore $h$ would have to satisfy
$$
h(x)=1 \quad \text{for } x\in(0,1),
$$
and
$$
h(x)=0 \quad \text{for } x<0.
$$
But then continuity at $x=0$ is impossible, since the left limit would be $0$
while the right limit would be $1$.

Applying this to $h=f*g$, we get a contradiction. Hence no such
$g\in L^1(\mathbb R)$ exists.
::

 

::ProblemBlock{number=6}
#problem
Let $f\in L^1(\mathbb R)$, and for all $z\in\mathbb R$ define
$$
g(z):=\sup_{r>0}\frac{1}{2r}\int_{z-r}^{z+r}|f(x)|\,dx.
$$
Prove that, for any $t>0$,
$$
|\{z:g(z)>t\}|\le \frac{5}{t}\|f\|_{L^1(\mathbb R)}.
$$

#proof
Fix $t>0$, and set
$$
E_t=\{z\in\mathbb R:g(z)>t\}.
$$
For each $z\in E_t$, by the definition of $g(z)$, there exists $r_z>0$ such
that
$$
\frac{1}{2r_z}\int_{z-r_z}^{z+r_z}|f(x)|\,dx>t.
$$
Let
$$
I_z=(z-r_z,z+r_z).
$$
Then
$$
|I_z|=2r_z,
$$
and the previous inequality becomes
$$
\int_{I_z}|f(x)|\,dx>t|I_z|.
$$
Equivalently,
$$
|I_z|<\frac{1}{t}\int_{I_z}|f(x)|\,dx.
$$

We now use the following [Vitali's covering lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitali_covering_lemma) from any finite collection
of intervals, one can choose a pairwise disjoint subcollection $I_1,\dots,I_N$
such that the union of the original intervals is contained in
$$
\bigcup_{k=1}^N 5I_k,
$$
where $5I_k$ denotes the interval with the same center as $I_k$ and five times
the length.

Now let $K\subset E_t$ be compact. The intervals $\{I_z:z\in K\}$ cover $K$,
so by compactness there is a finite subcover. Applying the covering lemma to
this finite collection, we obtain pairwise disjoint intervals
$I_1,\dots,I_N$ such that
$$
K\subset \bigcup_{k=1}^N 5I_k.
$$
Therefore
$$
|K|
\le
\sum_{k=1}^N |5I_k|
=
5\sum_{k=1}^N |I_k|.
$$
Using the estimate for each $I_k$, we get
$$
|K|
\le
5\sum_{k=1}^N |I_k|
<
\frac{5}{t}\sum_{k=1}^N \int_{I_k}|f(x)|\,dx.
$$
Since the intervals $I_1,\dots,I_N$ are pairwise disjoint,
$$
\sum_{k=1}^N \int_{I_k}|f(x)|\,dx
\le
\int_{\mathbb R}|f(x)|\,dx
=
\|f\|_{L^1(\mathbb R)}.
$$
Hence
$$
|K|\le \frac{5}{t}\|f\|_{L^1(\mathbb R)}.
$$

Since this holds for every compact set $K\subset E_t$, by inner regularity of
Lebesgue measure,
$$
|E_t|\le \frac{5}{t}\|f\|_{L^1(\mathbb R)}.
$$
Thus
$$
|\{z:g(z)>t\}|\le \frac{5}{t}\|f\|_{L^1(\mathbb R)}.
$$
::
