::ProblemBlock{number=1}
#problem
Let $f$ be a measurable function on $[0,1]$ such that $f(x)>0$ for all
$x\in[0,1]$, and assume that
$$
\int_{[0,1]} f(x)\,dx<\infty.
$$
Let $0<\gamma<1$. Prove that
$$
\inf_{|E|=\gamma}\int_E f(x)\,dx>0,
$$
where the infimum is taken over all measurable subsets $E\subset[0,1]$ of
Lebesgue measure $\gamma$.

#proof
For each $n\in\mathbb N$, define
$$
A_n=\left\{x\in[0,1]: f(x)\ge \frac1n\right\}.
$$
Since $f(x)>0$ for every $x\in[0,1]$, we have
$$
[0,1]=\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n.
$$
Also, the sets $A_n$ are increasing:
$$
A_1\subset A_2\subset A_3\subset \cdots.
$$
Therefore, by continuity from below of Lebesgue measure,
$$
|A_n|\to 1.
$$
Choose $N\in\mathbb N$ such that
$$
|A_N|>1-\frac{\gamma}{2}.
$$

Now let $E\subset[0,1]$ be measurable with $|E|=\gamma$. Then
$$
|E\cap A_N|
=
|E|+|A_N|-|E\cup A_N|.
$$
Since $E\cup A_N\subset[0,1]$, we have $|E\cup A_N|\le 1$. Hence
$$
|E\cap A_N|
\ge
\gamma+|A_N|-1
>
\gamma+\left(1-\frac{\gamma}{2}\right)-1
=
\frac{\gamma}{2}.
$$
On $A_N$, we have
$$
f(x)\ge \frac1N.
$$
Thus
$$
\int_E f(x)\,dx
\ge
\int_{E\cap A_N} f(x)\,dx
\ge
\frac1N |E\cap A_N|
>
\frac{\gamma}{2N}.
$$
Since this lower bound is independent of $E$, it follows that
$$
\inf_{|E|=\gamma}\int_E f(x)\,dx
\ge
\frac{\gamma}{2N}
>
0.
$$
This proves the claim.

::ProblemBlock{number=2}
#problem
Let
$$
\Omega=\{(x_1,x_2)\in\mathbb R^2:0<x_2<x_1\le 1\}.
$$
Let
$$
f(x)=\left(x_1^2+x_2^2\right)^{-b/2}
\left|\log\left(x_1^2+x_2^2\right)\right|^{-\gamma}.
$$
Determine for which values of $b\in\mathbb R$ and $\gamma\in\mathbb R$ the
Lebesgue integral
$$
\int_{\Omega} f(x)\,dx
$$
is finite.



#proof
Use polar coordinates
$$
x_1=r\cos\theta,
\qquad
x_2=r\sin\theta.
$$
The region $\Omega$ becomes
$$
0<\theta<\frac{\pi}{4},
\qquad
0<r<\sec\theta.
$$
Also,
$$
x_1^2+x_2^2=r^2,
$$
and $dx=r\,dr\,d\theta$. Therefore
$$
\int_{\Omega} f(x)\,dx
=
\int_0^{\pi/4}\int_0^{\sec\theta}
r^{-b}\left|\log(r^2)\right|^{-\gamma}r\,dr\,d\theta.
$$
Thus
$$
\int_{\Omega} f(x)\,dx
=
\int_0^{\pi/4}\int_0^{\sec\theta}
r^{1-b}\left|\log(r^2)\right|^{-\gamma}\,dr\,d\theta.
$$

Since $0<\theta<\pi/4$, the $\theta$-interval has finite length. Hence the
only possible singularities occur when
$$
r=0
$$
or when
$$
\log(r^2)=0,
$$
that is, when
$$
r=1.
$$

First consider the behavior near $r=0$. We need to study
$$
\int_0^{1/2} r^{1-b}|\log(r^2)|^{-\gamma}\,dr.
$$
As $r\to0$, set
$$
s=-\log(r^2).
$$
Then $s\to\infty$ and
$$
\frac{dr}{r}=-\frac12\,ds.
$$
If $b=2$, then the integral becomes comparable to
$$
\int^\infty s^{-\gamma}\,ds,
$$
which is finite if and only if
$$
\gamma>1.
$$
If $b<2$, then the factor $r^{1-b}$ is integrable near $0$, and the logarithmic
factor cannot destroy integrability. Hence the integral is finite for all
$\gamma$ when $b<2$.

If $b>2$, then $r^{1-b}$ is too singular near $0$, and the logarithmic factor
cannot restore integrability. Hence the integral diverges for all $\gamma$
when $b>2$.

So near $r=0$, integrability requires
$$
b<2
$$
or
$$
b=2 \text{ and } \gamma>1.
$$

Now consider the behavior near $r=1$. Since
$$
\log(r^2)=2\log r,
$$
and $\log r\sim r-1$ as $r\to1$, we have
$$
|\log(r^2)|\sim |r-1|.
$$
Also, near $r=1$, the factor $r^{1-b}$ is bounded above and below by positive
constants. Therefore the relevant integral is comparable to
$$
\int_{1-\delta}^{1+\delta} |r-1|^{-\gamma}\,dr.
$$
This integral is finite if and only if
$$
\gamma<1.
$$

Therefore we must have
$$
\gamma<1.
$$

Combining the two conditions, the case $b=2$ is impossible, because near
$r=0$ it would require $\gamma>1$, while near $r=1$ it requires $\gamma<1$.
Thus the only possible condition is
$$
b<2
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\gamma<1.
$$

Conversely, if
$$
b<2
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\gamma<1,
$$
then the integral is finite near $r=0$, finite near $r=1$, and bounded away
from both singularities. Hence
$$
\int_{\Omega} f(x)\,dx<\infty.
$$

Therefore,
$$
\boxed{
\int_{\Omega} f(x)\,dx<\infty
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
b<2 \text{ and } \gamma<1.
}
$$

::ProblemBlock{number=3}
#problem
Let $f\in L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}(\mathbb R^n)$, let $p\in(0,1)$, and let
$C_c(\mathbb R^n)$ be the set of continuous functions with compact support in
$\mathbb R^n$. Assume that
$$
\int_{\mathbb R^n} f(x)g(x)\,dx
\le
\left(\int_{\mathbb R^n}|g(x)|^p\,dx\right)^{1/p}
$$
for all $g\in C_c(\mathbb R^n)$.
Prove that
$$
f(x)=0
$$
for a.e. $x\in\mathbb R^n$.

#proof
First, applying the assumption to $g$ and to $-g$, we get
$$
\left|\int_{\mathbb R^n} f(x)g(x)\,dx\right|
\le
\left(\int_{\mathbb R^n}|g(x)|^p\,dx\right)^{1/p}
$$
for every $g\in C_c(\mathbb R^n)$.

We will show that
$$
\int_{\mathbb R^n} f(x)\varphi(x)\,dx=0
$$
for every $\varphi\in C_c(\mathbb R^n)$.

Fix $\varphi\in C_c(\mathbb R^n)$, and let
$$
K=\operatorname{supp}\varphi.
$$
Since $K$ is compact, it is contained in some cube $Q\subset\mathbb R^n$.
Let $\delta>0$. Cover $Q$ by finitely many cubes $Q_1,\dots,Q_N$ of side
length $\delta$, where
$$
N\le C\delta^{-n}.
$$
Choose a continuous partition of unity $\eta_1,\dots,\eta_N$ subordinate to
slightly enlarged cubes, such that
$$
0\le \eta_j\le 1,
\qquad
\sum_{j=1}^N \eta_j=1
\quad \text{on } K,
$$
and
$$
|\operatorname{supp}\eta_j|\le C\delta^n.
$$
Then
$$
\varphi=\sum_{j=1}^N \varphi\eta_j.
$$
Each $\varphi\eta_j$ belongs to $C_c(\mathbb R^n)$. Therefore,
$$
\left|\int_{\mathbb R^n} f\varphi\,dx\right|
\le
\sum_{j=1}^N
\left|\int_{\mathbb R^n} f\varphi\eta_j\,dx\right|.
$$
Using the assumed estimate, we get
$$
\left|\int_{\mathbb R^n} f\varphi\,dx\right|
\le
\sum_{j=1}^N
\left(\int_{\mathbb R^n}|\varphi\eta_j|^p\,dx\right)^{1/p}.
$$
Since $|\eta_j|\le1$,
$$
|\varphi\eta_j|\le \|\varphi\|_\infty.
$$
Thus
$$
\left(\int_{\mathbb R^n}|\varphi\eta_j|^p\,dx\right)^{1/p}
\le
\|\varphi\|_\infty |\operatorname{supp}\eta_j|^{1/p}.
$$
Hence
$$
\left|\int_{\mathbb R^n} f\varphi\,dx\right|
\le
\|\varphi\|_\infty
\sum_{j=1}^N |\operatorname{supp}\eta_j|^{1/p}.
$$
Using the estimates on the supports,
$$
\sum_{j=1}^N |\operatorname{supp}\eta_j|^{1/p}
\le
C\delta^{-n}(C\delta^n)^{1/p}
=
C\delta^{n(\frac1p-1)}.
$$
Since $0<p<1$, we have
$$
\frac1p-1>0.
$$
Therefore,
$$
\delta^{n(\frac1p-1)}\to0
\quad\text{as}\quad
\delta\to0.
$$
It follows that
$$
\left|\int_{\mathbb R^n} f\varphi\,dx\right|=0.
$$
Thus
$$
\int_{\mathbb R^n} f(x)\varphi(x)\,dx=0
$$
for every $\varphi\in C_c(\mathbb R^n)$.

Since $f\in L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}(\mathbb R^n)$ and its integral against every
continuous compactly supported test function is zero, we conclude that
$$
f=0
$$
almost everywhere on $\mathbb R^n$.

::ProblemBlock{number=4}
#problem
Let $f\in C_c(\mathbb R)$. Show that there is a sequence
$g_n\in BV(\mathbb R)$ with
$$
TV(g_n)=1
$$
such that
$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}\int_{\mathbb R} f(x)g_n'(x)\,dx
=
\sup_{x\in\mathbb R}|f(x)|
=
\|f\|_\infty.
$$

#proof
Since $f\in C_c(\mathbb R)$, $f$ is continuous and has compact support.
Therefore $|f|$ attains its maximum. Choose $x_0\in\mathbb R$ such that
$$
|f(x_0)|=\|f\|_\infty.
$$

If $f\equiv 0$, the conclusion is immediate. So assume $f\not\equiv0$ and set
$$
\sigma=\frac{f(x_0)}{|f(x_0)|}.
$$
Thus $\sigma\in\{-1,1\}$ and
$$
\sigma f(x_0)=|f(x_0)|=\|f\|_\infty.
$$

Choose a nonnegative function $\rho\in C_c^\infty((-1,1))$ such that
$$
\int_{\mathbb R}\rho(x)\,dx=1.
$$
Define
$$
\rho_n(x)=n\rho(n(x-x_0)).
$$
Then
$$
\rho_n\ge0,
\qquad
\int_{\mathbb R}\rho_n(x)\,dx=1,
$$
and $\rho_n$ concentrates at $x_0$ as $n\to\infty$.

Now define
$$
g_n(x)=\sigma\int_{-\infty}^x \rho_n(t)\,dt.
$$
Then $g_n$ is absolutely continuous, hence $g_n\in BV(\mathbb R)$, and
$$
g_n'(x)=\sigma\rho_n(x).
$$
Therefore
$$
TV(g_n)
=
\int_{\mathbb R}|g_n'(x)|\,dx
=
\int_{\mathbb R}|\sigma|\rho_n(x)\,dx
=
1.
$$

Finally,
$$
\int_{\mathbb R} f(x)g_n'(x)\,dx
=
\sigma\int_{\mathbb R} f(x)\rho_n(x)\,dx.
$$
Since $f$ is continuous at $x_0$ and $\rho_n$ is an approximate identity
concentrating at $x_0$, we have
$$
\int_{\mathbb R} f(x)\rho_n(x)\,dx\to f(x_0).
$$
Hence
$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}\int_{\mathbb R} f(x)g_n'(x)\,dx
=
\sigma f(x_0)
=
|f(x_0)|
=
\|f\|_\infty.
$$

Thus there exists a sequence $g_n\in BV(\mathbb R)$ with $TV(g_n)=1$ such that
$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}\int_{\mathbb R} f(x)g_n'(x)\,dx
=
\|f\|_\infty.
$$
::

::ProblemBlock{number=5}
#problem
Let $E\subset\mathbb R$ have finite outer measure. Prove that $E$ is
Lebesgue measurable if and only if for each open bounded interval $(a,b)$,
$$
b-a
=
m^*((a,b)\cap E)+m^*((a,b)\setminus E).
$$

#proof
 First suppose that $E$ is Lebesgue measurable. Since every open interval
$(a,b)$ is measurable, the sets
$$
(a,b)\cap E
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
(a,b)\setminus E
$$
are measurable and disjoint. Therefore, by additivity of Lebesgue measure,
$$
m((a,b))
=
m((a,b)\cap E)+m((a,b)\setminus E).
$$
Since $m((a,b))=b-a$ and measurable sets have measure equal to outer measure,
we get
$$
b-a
=
m^*((a,b)\cap E)+m^*((a,b)\setminus E).
$$

Conversely, assume that for every bounded open interval $(a,b)$,
$$
b-a
=
m^*((a,b)\cap E)+m^*((a,b)\setminus E).
$$
We prove that $E$ is Lebesgue measurable by verifying Carathéodory's criterion.

Let $A\subset\mathbb R$ be arbitrary. We need to show that
$$
m^*(A)
=
m^*(A\cap E)+m^*(A\setminus E).
$$
By subadditivity of outer measure, we always have
$$
m^*(A)
\le
m^*(A\cap E)+m^*(A\setminus E).
$$
Thus it remains to prove the reverse inequality.

If $m^*(A)=\infty$, there is nothing to prove. So assume
$$
m^*(A)<\infty.
$$
Let $\varepsilon>0$. By the definition of outer measure, there exist bounded
open intervals $I_k=(a_k,b_k)$ such that
$$
A\subset \bigcup_{k=1}^{\infty} I_k
$$
and
$$
\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} |I_k|
<
m^*(A)+\varepsilon.
$$
For each $k$, by assumption,
$$
|I_k|
=
m^*(I_k\cap E)+m^*(I_k\setminus E).
$$
Since
$$
A\cap E\subset \bigcup_{k=1}^{\infty}(I_k\cap E),
$$
we have
$$
m^*(A\cap E)
\le
\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}m^*(I_k\cap E).
$$
Similarly,
$$
A\setminus E\subset \bigcup_{k=1}^{\infty}(I_k\setminus E),
$$
so
$$
m^*(A\setminus E)
\le
\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}m^*(I_k\setminus E).
$$
Therefore,
$$
m^*(A\cap E)+m^*(A\setminus E)
\le
\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}
\left[
m^*(I_k\cap E)+m^*(I_k\setminus E)
\right].
$$
Using the assumed equality on each interval $I_k$, we get
$$
m^*(A\cap E)+m^*(A\setminus E)
\le
\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}|I_k|
<
m^*(A)+\varepsilon.
$$
Since $\varepsilon>0$ was arbitrary,
$$
m^*(A\cap E)+m^*(A\setminus E)
\le
m^*(A).
$$
Together with the opposite inequality from subadditivity, this gives
$$
m^*(A)
=
m^*(A\cap E)+m^*(A\setminus E)
$$
for every $A\subset\mathbb R$.

Hence, by Carathéodory's criterion, $E$ is Lebesgue measurable.
::

 

::ProblemBlock{number=6}
#problem
Assume that
$$
\int_0^1 |f_n(x)|^3\,dx\le 1,
\qquad n\in\mathbb N,
$$
where $f_n:[0,1]\to\mathbb R$ are functions such that
$$
f_n(x)\to0
$$
pointwise almost everywhere as $n\to\infty$. Show that
$$
\|f_n\|_2\to0
$$
as $n\to\infty$.

#proof
We want to prove that
$$
\int_0^1 |f_n(x)|^2\,dx\to0.
$$

Let $M>0$. Split the integral as
$$
\int_0^1 |f_n|^2\,dx
=
\int_{\{|f_n|\le M\}} |f_n|^2\,dx
+
\int_{\{|f_n|>M\}} |f_n|^2\,dx.
$$

On the set $\{|f_n|\le M\}$, we have
$$
|f_n|^2\le M^2.
$$
Also,
$$
|f_n|^2\mathbf 1_{\{|f_n|\le M\}}\to0
$$
pointwise almost everywhere. Therefore, 
$$
\int_{\{|f_n|\le M\}} |f_n|^2\,dx\to0
$$
for each fixed $M>0$.

On the set $\{|f_n|>M\}$, we have
$$
|f_n|^2\le \frac{1}{M}|f_n|^3.
$$
Hence
$$
\int_{\{|f_n|>M\}} |f_n|^2\,dx
\le
\frac1M\int_0^1 |f_n|^3\,dx
\le
\frac1M.
$$

Thus
$$
\limsup_{n\to\infty}\int_0^1 |f_n|^2\,dx
\le
\frac1M.
$$
Since $M>0$ is arbitrary, letting $M\to\infty$ gives
$$
\limsup_{n\to\infty}\int_0^1 |f_n|^2\,dx=0.
$$
Therefore
$$
\int_0^1 |f_n|^2\,dx\to0.
$$
Hence
$$
\|f_n\|_2\to0.
$$
::
